The Fun in Building an App and Submitting to iTunes (Download)

The source code is from a game called, “Jump Car.” I used Unity3D and Xcode, and quite a bit of Photoshop in order to reskin the game, update it for AdMob and submit it to iTunes. I replaced the cars with the jeepney—a popular means of transportation in the Philippines (where I grew up), and named my game “Jumpy Jeepney.” Rebuilding the app from the Jump Car source code was relatively easy, although the parts where I got stuck was debugging through the errors in xCode. After the first few compiles, I would get over 200 errors, and that’s when pair programming was useful and I enlisted the help of my friend Jon over at Tag Arcade.

The most useful resources to learn how to get your Unity3D mobile game into the app store really varies in your learning style. I found Unity 3.x Game Development Essentials to be extremely useful for referencing, and most of all, reading a ton of code online for examples also helped me. Understanding Objective-C and Swift will make life much easier when you are debugging, and if you don’t really understand it while reskinning a clone, it can take much longer than what they say in articles like Wired or websites like Chupamobile that sell source code.
